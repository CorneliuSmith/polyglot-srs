# Curriculum reference — Swahili (sw)

The app's grammar path in teaching order. These point titles are exactly what the learner sees on their cards, and weak grammar items arrive labeled with them — use the same names when coaching, and sequence new material in this order.

## A1
- Personal pronouns and ni (to be) — Introduce yourself and others
- Subject prefixes (ni-, u-, a-…) — Make the verb agree with who is acting
- Present tense -na- — Say what is happening now or generally
- Noun classes: M-/Wa- (people) — Talk about people, one or many
- Noun classes: Ki-/Vi- (things) — Talk about everyday objects
- Present negative (si-…-i) — Say what is not happening
- Past tense -li- — Say what happened
- Future tense -ta- — Say what will happen
- Question words (nini, nani, wapi) — Ask what, who, and where
- Adjective agreement — Describe people and things

## A2
- Perfect -me- — Say what has (just) happened
- Object infixes (-ku-, -m-, -wa-…) — Say who or what the action is done to
- Noun classes: Ma- (class 5/6) — Use the ji-/ma- noun family
- Noun classes: N- (class 9/10) — Use the N-class — same shape, singular and plural
- Possessives (-angu, -ako, -ake…) — Say whose something is
- The -a of association (wa, ya, cha…) — Link two nouns ('of')
- To have: kuwa na — Say what people have
- Imperatives and polite requests — Tell and ask people to do things

## B1
- Subjunctive -e — Express wants, purposes, and suggestions
- Negative past and future — Deny things in any tense
- Relative clauses (amba- and -ye-/-o-) — Say 'who/which' to describe things
- Locatives (-ni, kuna/pana/mna) — Talk about places and what is in them
- Habitual hu- — Talk about routines and general truths
- Kuwa in other tenses (alikuwa, atakuwa) — Say how things were and will be
- Noun class U- (class 11/14: uhuru, ukuta) — Talk about abstracts and long thin things
- Noun class Ku- (class 15: kusoma as a noun) — Use whole actions as subjects
- Place classes 16–18 (mahali pa-, ku-, mu-) — Agree with places, not just point at them
- Demonstratives (huyu / hiki / hii… and -le) — Point at things in any noun class
- Comparatives (kuliko, zaidi ya, kama) — Compare people and things

## B2
- Passive -wa — Say what was done (without the doer)
- Causative -isha/-esha — Say you make something happen
- Applicative -ia/-ea — Do something FOR or TO someone
- Stative -ika/-eka — Say something got into a state (or is doable)
- Reciprocal -ana — Say people do things to each other
- Relative of time -po- (nilipofika) — Say when something happened in one word
- Relative of manner -vyo- (unavyojua) — Say how — 'the way that…'
- Reported speech (kwamba, kuwa, eti) — Pass on what someone said
- Compound tenses (alikuwa akisoma / amesoma) — Set one action inside another time
- Reversive -ua / -oa (funga → fungua) — Undo a verb with one suffix

## C1
- Conditional -ki- (if/when) — Make real conditions
- Hypothetical -nge- / -ngali- — Imagine what would (have) happen(ed)
- Narrative -ka- — Tell a story, action after action
- Concessives (ingawa, ijapokuwa, licha ya) — Concede a point and push past it
- Emphatic ndi- (ndiye, ndipo, ndivyo) — Say 'that's exactly the one / when / how'
- Negative conditional -sipo- (unless) — Warn about what happens if not
- Purpose clauses: ili + subjunctive — Say what something is FOR

## C2
- Participial -ki- (waliondoka wakiimba) — Paint two actions in one stroke
- Kupiga idioms (kupiga simu, picha, hodi) — Sound native with the 'hit' verb
- Methali — proverbs and their grammar — Deploy proverbs the way elders do
- Respect register (shikamoo, honorific plural) — Show respect the Swahili way
